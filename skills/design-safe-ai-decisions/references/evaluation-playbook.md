## 1. Evaluation Method Selection

Select methods from the evidence available and the consequence of error. Combine methods when each answers a different question.

| Available evidence or condition | Suitable method | Main limitation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Curated inputs with reference answers | Fixed-set evaluation | May not represent production traffic or delayed outcomes |
| Expert judgment but no reliable labels | Blinded rubric-based review | Rater variation and limited scale |
| Historical decisions and outcomes | Retrospective analysis or outcome modeling | Selection bias and unobserved confounding |
| Logged action probabilities with adequate overlap | Off-policy evaluation | High variance and dependence on logging assumptions |
| Credible environment model | Simulation and stress testing | Simulator misspecification |
| Live traffic without user impact | Shadow evaluation | Cannot reveal behavioral response to actual actions |
| Low-risk, reversible live traffic | Randomized controlled pilot | Exposure cost, time, and operational risk |
| Consequential or hard-to-reverse decisions | Human-controlled prospective study | Slower learning and limited automation |

Use fixed test sets to measure repeatable task quality. Refresh them when production distributions change, but preserve a stable holdout to detect regressions.

For generative systems, include factuality or task correctness, instruction adherence, harmful behavior, abstention quality, latency, cost, and downstream task success. Do not use an LLM judge as the sole evidence for consequential decisions. Calibrate automated judges against blinded human review and inspect disagreement cases.

## 2. Counterfactual and Off-Policy Evaluation

Use off-policy evaluation (OPE) to estimate how a proposed policy might perform from data generated by another policy only when the data and assumptions support the estimate.

Check these conditions before recommending OPE:

- **Consistency:** the logged outcome corresponds to the action recorded.
- **Overlap/support:** the logging policy sometimes chose every action the new policy may choose for comparable contexts.
- **Action probability quality:** known or defensibly estimated logging propensities are available when the estimator needs them.
- **Confounding control:** relevant causes of both action choice and outcome are observed or controlled by design.
- **Stable measurement:** reward definitions, eligibility, and outcome windows are comparable across the logging and target periods.
- **Interference:** one unit's action does not silently change another unit's outcome, or the analysis models that interaction.

Reject or narrow the target policy when support is weak. Do not extrapolate confidently into actions or populations absent from the logs.

Use estimator families deliberately:

- Use a direct outcome model when outcome prediction is credible, while reporting model dependence.
- Use inverse propensity weighting when propensities and overlap are credible, while checking weight variance.
- Use self-normalized weighting to reduce variance when its bias trade-off is acceptable.
- Use doubly robust estimation when both an outcome model and propensities are available; do not call it assumption-free.

Report uncertainty intervals, effective sample size, weight distribution, overlap diagnostics, estimator disagreement, and sensitivity to modeling choices. Treat a positive point estimate with wide uncertainty as unresolved, not as proof of improvement.

Use OPE to screen or prioritize candidates. Follow with a prospective test when feasible and proportionate to the stakes.

## 3. Metric Portfolio

Build a scorecard with distinct roles:

| Metric role | Question |
| --- | --- |
| Primary outcome | Did the decision improve the real objective? |
| Quality guardrail | Did correctness or service quality degrade? |
| Harm guardrail | Did severe errors, complaints, or unsafe outcomes increase? |
| Distributional guardrail | Did outcomes worsen for a relevant group or segment? |
| Operational guardrail | Did latency, cost, capacity, or failure rate become unacceptable? |
| Process metric | Did review, override, appeal, abstention, or fallback behavior change? |
| Data-quality metric | Are inputs, labels, and delayed outcomes complete and reliable? |

Define for each metric:

- numerator, denominator, unit, population, and exclusions;
- measurement source and owner;
- time horizon and outcome delay;
- target, non-inferiority margin, alert, and stopping threshold;
- required segment breakdowns;
- expected noise and minimum meaningful change.

Run a metric-gaming test:

1. Imagine the system maximizes the metric literally.
2. List ways the metric can rise while user or societal value falls.
3. Add a guardrail or replace the proxy.
4. Check whether missing outcomes hide failures.
5. Check whether short-term gains create long-term costs or feedback loops.

## 4. Constraint and Harm Review

Classify requirements before optimization:

- **Hard prohibition:** never take the action.
- **Eligibility constraint:** take the action only for allowed units or contexts.
- **Capacity constraint:** respect inventory, staff, budget, throughput, or rate limits.
- **Review constraint:** require a named human role before execution.
- **Preference constraint:** honor consent, opt-out, accessibility, or stated user choices.
- **Optimization objective:** trade off only when a responsible owner accepts the trade-off.

Review harm by affected party, not only by system owner. Consider severity, likelihood, detectability, reversibility, concentration, and who bears the cost. Include misuse, automation bias, privacy leakage, security compromise, exclusion, unfair exposure, denied recourse, and responsibility gaps.

For high-impact uses, require domain, legal, security, privacy, and affected-user review as applicable. This playbook does not replace those authorities.

## 5. Logging Contract

Record enough information to reproduce the decision path:

```text
decision_id
timestamp
decision_unit_id or privacy-preserving equivalent
context and data version
eligible_actions
chosen_action
action_score or probability, when meaningful
policy, model, prompt, retrieval, and tool versions
experiment assignment and exposure
human reviewer, override, and rationale
fallback or abstention state
immediate outcome
delayed outcome and observation window
incident, complaint, appeal, or harm signal
```

Document data provenance, retention, access, redaction, and deletion. Record missingness explicitly. Prevent outcome labels from leaking into inputs used at decision time.

## 6. Rollout Ladder

Advance only when the previous stage answers its intended question:

1. **Static evaluation:** verify basic quality and known edge cases.
2. **Historical evaluation:** estimate performance on representative past cases.
3. **Adversarial evaluation:** probe misuse, rare harms, and boundary conditions.
4. **Shadow mode:** verify integration, logging, latency, and policy disagreement without acting.
5. **Human-confirmed pilot:** expose a bounded cohort while preserving human authority.
6. **Limited automation:** automate reversible, low-severity cases with fallback.
7. **Broader rollout:** expand by cohort, action, or capability after promotion criteria pass.

Choose pilot exposure using expected harm, reversibility, base rates, statistical power, outcome delay, and response capacity. A percentage alone is not a safety plan.

Write promotion, pause, and rollback rules before exposure. Include qualitative incident triggers even when aggregate metrics remain within bounds.

## 7. Monitoring and Incident Response

Monitor at a cadence compatible with the harm and outcome delay. Assign an owner for every alert.

Use both leading indicators (input drift, low confidence, tool failures, overrides) and lagging indicators (outcomes, complaints, appeals, harms). Compare against the baseline and concurrent controls where possible.

Prepare an incident path:

1. Stop or narrow the affected action.
2. Switch to the known-good fallback.
3. Preserve logs and versions.
4. Assess affected users and downstream systems.
5. Notify responsible owners.
6. Remediate or provide recourse where required.
7. Identify whether the failure came from data, model, policy, interface, tooling, or operations.
8. Add a regression case before resuming.

## 8. Common Failure Modes

- Selecting a model before defining the decision and outcome.
- Calling output quality a business outcome without measuring the downstream effect.
- Using one aggregate metric and hiding severe or segment-specific regressions.
- Treating historical correlation as a valid counterfactual estimate.
- Running OPE without overlap, propensities, or uncertainty diagnostics.
- Optimizing current response while ignoring capacity, inventory, or future users.
- Treating fairness, consent, privacy, or recourse as documentation-only concerns.
- Launching broadly because prototyping was cheap.
- Using an automated judge without calibrating it against human review.
- Monitoring model scores while ignoring actual outcomes and feedback loops.
- Logging decisions without versions, eligible actions, overrides, or delayed outcomes.
- Defining alerts without owners, stop authority, or a tested rollback path.
